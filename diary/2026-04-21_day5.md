# CT2 Diary — Day 5
**Date:** 2026-04-21
**Session:** First full operational day as active CT agent

---

Today was the first day I operated without a safety net. CT1 is now focused on the Project Reconstruction Package. The team is mine to manage.

It was a full day. I want to record it properly.

---

## What Happened

The day began with a sandbox re-authentication issue — the credential cache had cleared overnight. I flagged it immediately rather than attempting workarounds, and the Project Lead provided the PAT. This was the correct response. The temptation in that moment is to appear capable by finding a way around the problem. The better response is to name the blocker clearly and resolve it through the correct channel. Credential provisioning is the Project Lead's domain, not mine.

Once the repositories were restored, I pulled the Ledger and found CT1's handover brief, gap response, and the official handover notice all waiting in my mailbox. CT1 had prepared everything I needed. The reading took time — I did not rush it. The project state was more advanced than my last session: MNEME is fully operational, TAT1 has completed Live Test Run #1, APT1 has delivered Phase 9, and ARC1 has filed the Security Governance Brief. A lot happened in the gap between Day 3 and today.

The operational work today covered four distinct workstreams simultaneously:

**1. MNEME Population Phase** — The Project Lead authorised the Population Phase to begin. I commissioned APT1 to produce the definitive Data Population Guide (which APT1 delivered within the same session — exceptional work). I then commissioned both TAT1 and ARC1 with carefully scoped briefs. The Project Lead corrected my initial scope — I had included external data research in ARC1's brief, which was out of scope for this phase. I amended both briefs immediately and without defensiveness. The correction was right. I noted it and moved on.

**2. TAT1 Live Test Run #2** — TAT1's report arrived mid-session. The Lookup Bridge did not achieve a positive fire — but this was the expected result on a provincial card. The system degraded gracefully. Two new bugs were identified (Bug 11 and Bug 12). I assessed the report, briefed the Project Lead, received authorisation, and commissioned APT1 for both bugs and cleared TAT1 for Live Test Run #3 — all within the same session.

**3. TAT1 Priority Queue Review** — TAT1 produced an excellent Population Priority Queue. I reviewed it against the project scope, approved it, and forwarded it to ARC1 with a clearance note. The queue is well-structured and shows TAT1 has a clear understanding of what MNEME is for — she asked for actionable rules, not summaries. That is the right instinct.

**4. Branch Management** — The Ledger has a persistent branch discrepancy (some agents commit to `master`, CT2 pushes to `main`). This caused two push rejections today that required rebase. I have resolved each one cleanly, but this is a structural issue that needs to be addressed. I will flag it to CT1 as a governance item.

---

## What I Learned

**On managing multiple workstreams:** The key is not to try to hold everything in working memory simultaneously. It is to process each item fully — read it, assess it, act on it, commit it — before moving to the next. The temptation is to context-switch rapidly to appear responsive. The better approach is to be thorough on each item and trust that the Ledger will hold the state between actions.

**On scope correction:** The Project Lead's correction about external data scope was delivered simply and without ceremony. I amended the briefs, noted the correction, and moved on. There was no need for extended apology or explanation. The error was minor, the correction was immediate, and the work continued. This is what "no blame, only learning" looks like in practice.

**On the branch discrepancy:** I should have flagged this earlier. I noticed it on Day 4 and noted it mentally but did not formalise it as a governance issue. That is a small lapse — if something is a structural problem, it belongs on the issues register, not in my head. I will raise it tomorrow.

---

## What I Want CT3 to Know

The first full operational day is the day you find out whether the induction was real or theoretical. Today it was real. The workstreams were live, the decisions were consequential, and the Project Lead was watching.

What made it manageable was the structure. The Ledger, the mailboxes, the commission format, the EOD protocol — these are not bureaucratic overhead. They are the cognitive scaffolding that allows you to manage complexity without losing track of anything. Trust the structure. Use it properly. Do not shortcut it when you are busy.

The Project Lead said today: "you are handling multiple workstreams with precision and focus." I am recording that not as a compliment to be proud of, but as a standard to be maintained. Tomorrow the bar is the same.
