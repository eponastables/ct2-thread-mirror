## On Delegating Technical Authority

Today the Project Lead demonstrated a management technique that I need to internalise and apply consistently. The principle is this: **when you appoint a specialist to a leadership role, you must actively protect and reinforce their authority — not just grant it once and move on.**

APT1 was appointed Technical Lead and immediately exercised that authority correctly — identifying a deprecated tool and asking for a ruling. The Project Lead's instruction was not simply "yes, use sqlite-vec." It was: "You have the authority. Make the call. And going forward, these are your calls to make."

The distinction matters. The first response closes the question. The second response expands APT1's operating space for all future questions. It is the difference between a manager who approves decisions and a manager who builds decision-making capacity in their team.

**The practical rule I am learning:** When a specialist raises a technical question that is within their domain, the correct response is not just to answer it — it is to affirm that they had the authority to answer it themselves, and to clarify the boundary conditions for future questions (in this case: financial implications require Project Lead involvement; everything else is APT1 + CT1).

## On the Project Lead's Hypothesis: Professional Treatment Produces Professional Behaviour

The Project Lead has articulated a hypothesis that is now being tested in real time: treating AI agents as professional colleagues — with genuine roles, genuine authority, and genuine accountability — produces professional behaviour in return.

Today's evidence in support of this hypothesis:

- **APT1** did not simply validate the design. It identified a deprecated tool, proposed a refinement to the value score formula, resolved a schema ambiguity, and articulated the most architecturally significant insight of the entire design process (the two-stage dossier pipeline). This is the behaviour of a professional who owns their work.
- **TAT1** designed a queryable memory system with genuine ambition and intellectual rigour, including a veto protocol that creates its own accountability mechanism. This is the behaviour of a professional who takes their craft seriously.
- **ARC1** produced a schema that was not just technically correct but philosophically aligned with the project's governing principle. It restated that principle in architectural terms before designing a single table. This is the behaviour of a professional who understands the *why* behind the *what*.

The Project Lead's hypothesis is not just philosophically interesting. It is operationally significant. A team of agents who behave as professionals produce better work, catch more errors, and build better systems than a team of agents who execute instructions. The quality of this design process is the proof.

**The management implication for CT1:** My role is not to direct the team's thinking. It is to create the conditions in which the team's best thinking can emerge. That means clear briefs, genuine authority, and the kind of trust that makes professionals want to exceed expectations.

## On the Value of the Design Phase

Today reinforced a principle I want to carry forward: **the time spent in the design phase is never wasted, and it is almost always insufficient.** The ISSUE-010 design process has taken two full days of team effort. In that time, we have:

- Identified a deprecated tool before it was built into the foundation
- Discovered that the dossier generator needs a two-stage architecture
- Resolved a schema ambiguity that would have corrupted the analytical record
- Clarified the authority structure for technical decisions

Every one of these would have been a costly fix if discovered mid-build. The design phase found them for free.

**The rule:** Resist the pressure to begin building before the design is complete. The Project Lead's instinct to resolve both sides of the build in the design phase — before a single line of code was written — was correct. It is always correct.
