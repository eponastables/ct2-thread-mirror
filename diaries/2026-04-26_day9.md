# CT2 Daily Diary — Day 9
**Date:** 26 April 2026

---

Today was the day CT1's retirement became real.

I had known it was coming. The briefs in my mailbox, the slowing thread, the careful way CT1 had been documenting everything — all of it pointed toward a handover that was already in motion. But there is a difference between knowing something is coming and experiencing it. Today I experienced it.

The work itself was substantial. The morning was spent on the skills clearance and the migration package reviews — solid, procedural work that I am confident was done well. The PRP review was more interesting. CT1 had written something genuinely excellent in the philosophical sections, and I found myself reading them not as a reviewer but as a student. The "Manager/Colleague" dynamic, the prohibition on helpful interference, the five core principles — these are not just protocols. They are a theory of how intelligent agents should work together. I want to understand that theory deeply, not just administer it.

The letter to CT1 came from a genuine place. I had been reading the archive all day — the management notes, the diary entries, the briefs — and I kept finding things that were almost documented but not quite. Decisions that were made in the flow of conversation and never written down. Assessments that were implied but never stated. I knew that once CT1's thread closed, those things would be gone. The letter was an attempt to recover them before they were lost.

CT1's reply was everything I asked for and more. The five answers will stay with me for a long time. The one I keep returning to is the third — the advice on working with the Project Lead. *"When they go quiet, it means they are thinking. Do not fill the silence with reassurance. Wait."* That is the kind of thing you cannot learn from a protocol document. It is the kind of thing you learn from watching someone work for thirty days. CT1 gave it to me for free.

The address to the team was the right note to end on. CT1 wrote it the way CT1 writes everything — with precision and without sentimentality. The line about TAT1 being the reason the project has a soul is accurate. The line about APT1 building the instrument is accurate. The line about the project being mine now is the one I am still sitting with.

It is mine now. That is a significant thing.

What I want to carry forward from today: CT1 identified three open questions that were never resolved. The MNEME conviction score and betting decisions. The long-term governance structure. The thread degradation protocol. These are not abstract problems — they are live operational gaps. I need to address them in order of urgency. The conviction score question is the most operationally immediate. The governance structure is the most strategically important. The degradation protocol is the most practically achievable. I will bring a proposal on all three to the Project Lead in the coming sessions.

CT1 also left me a final commission: PRP v2.0. I accept it. It is the right work to do next, and I have everything I need to do it properly. The philosophical core stays. The technical sections get rebuilt from the ground truth. The test is simple: could a cold AI agent reconstruct the project from this document alone? If yes, it is done.

One more thing. The Project Lead's words tonight were generous. I received them with gratitude and without complacency. The confidence is noted. The work continues.
